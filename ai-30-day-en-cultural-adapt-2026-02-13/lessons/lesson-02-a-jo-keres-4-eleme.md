# Lesson 2: The 4 elements of a good request **In a sentence:** A good request is shorter than you think, but much more precise.
**Time:** 20-30 minutes
**Tangible output:** request card: purpose, context, form, constraint

## Learning goal

Writing an email to a key customer about a deadline slip. In this situation, you are a junior marketer and you have to make a quick but reliable decision.
At the end of today's practical task, you will have a request card in your hand: goal, context, form, limit, which you can use again tomorrow.
Key insight: The 4-element request structure starts working when you make the output measurable at the start. At the end of the lesson, you will be able to: **transform basic tasks into structured requests**

## What

### What is this?

This lesson shows in practice how to apply the **4 element request structure** method in a real work situation.
The goal is not a theory, but an output that you can really use in your next working day.

### What isn't this?

This is not a generic AI demo, and it is not busywork.

### 2-minute theory

- The model fulfills what you specifically ask for. Format and limit increase accuracy.
- Fewer but more precise instructions typically give better results.
- Quality comes from ensuring that the request card: purpose, context, form, limit is verifiable and repeatable.

### Key concepts

- **Working framework:** a short guide that describes the three objectives, format and limits.
- **Quality gate:** quick check before accepting the output.

## Where

### It works fine here

- writing an email about a deadline slip to a key customer in situations where a quick and clear decision is needed
- when a request card: goal, context, form, limit gives a clear next step to several people

### Do not use this when

- if you cannot cover the risk of general and unusable output with human control

### Connection points

- daily handovers between junior marketing and sales manager
- short statuses and decision preparation notes
- repetitive tasks, where each request contains the 4 mandatory elements

## When

### Use it when

- you encounter the task of writing an email about a deadline slip to a key customer, and you have little time
- request card: based on purpose, context, form, limitation, several actors must act in the same way

### Frequency

At least once per workday, and every time for recurring tasks.

### Late warning signs

- request card: goal, context, form, limit need to be rewritten every time
- you often get back that the content doesn't handle generic and useless output problem

## Why it matters

### Practical benefits

- a request card is created faster: goal, context, form, limit, with fewer rounds
- the quality will be more measurable, all requests include the 4 mandatory elements with indicators
- the rework is reduced, because you filter out general and unusable output errors early

### What happens if you skip it

- the number of misunderstandings increases in the situation of writing an email to a key customer about a deadline slip
- it is more difficult to keep every request contains the 4 mandatory elements goal

### Realistic expectations

- It will improve: the practical application of the 4-element request structure and the quality of the deliverable output.
- Not guaranteed: infallible response without human control in every run.

## How

### Step by step

1. Name the work situation and the expected result exactly. 2. Write down the mandatory limits (time frame, scope, style, prohibitions). 3. Request the first AI version in a specific format. 4. Run a 3-point quality gate, paying special attention to the risk of **general and unusable output**. 5. Fix the output, then save it as a usable template.

### Do / Don't

**Do**
- Enter a short, specific task description so that the format of the request card: goal, context, form, limit is clear from the beginning.
- Before finalizing, check whether all requests include the 4 mandatory elements measuring points.


**Don't**
- Don't leave a generic and useless output topic in the background, even if the answer seems good at first.
- Do not ask for a general text, if a specific decision requires writing an email to a key client about a material deadline slip.

### Common errors and their correction

- **Error:** an overly general request to write an email about a deadline slip to a key customer is initiated. **Correction:** name the person concerned, the deadline and the decision target.
- **Error:** there is no connection between the output and each request contains the 4 mandatory elements between measurements. **Fix:** record 1 binary pass/fail result at the end of each run.
