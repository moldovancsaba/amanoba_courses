## What

### What is this?

This lesson shows in practice how to apply the **thinking table** method in a real work situation.
The goal is not a theory, but an output that you can really use in your next working day.

### What isn't this?

This is not a generic AI demo, and it is not busywork.

### 2-minute theory

- The structure reduces distortion. There is no fair comparison without uniform criteria.
- The key is that you always translate the task into a clear output.
- The quality here is provided by the fact that the comparative decision table is verifiable and repeatable.

### Key concepts

- **Working framework:** a short guide that describes the three objectives, format and limits.
- **Quality gate:** quick check before accepting the output.

## Where

### It works fine here

- Comparison of 3 supplier offers in situations where a quick and clear decision is required
- when a comparative decision board gives several people a clear next step

### Do not use this when

- if you cannot cover the risk of mixing aspects with human control

### Connection points

- daily handovers between procurement staff and financial analyst
- short statuses and decision preparation notes
- repetitive tasks, where at least 4 common evaluation criteria matter

## When

### Use it when

- You are faced with the task of comparing 3 supplier offers, and time is short
- based on a comparative decision table, several actors must act in the same way

### Frequency

At least once per workday, and every time for recurring tasks.

### Late warning signs

- comparative decision table needs to be rewritten every time
- you often get back that the content does not handle the problem of mixing aspects

## Why it matters

### Practical benefits

- a comparative decision board is prepared faster, with fewer rounds
- quality will be more measurable with at least 4 common evaluation criteria indicators
- the rework is reduced, because you filter out errors of mixing aspects early

### What happens if you skip it

- the number of misunderstandings increases in the situation of comparing 3 supplier offers
- it is more difficult to maintain at least 4 common evaluation criteria goals

### Realistic expectation
- It will improve: the practical application of tabular thinking and the quality of the transferable output.
- Not guaranteed: infallible response without human control in every run.

## How

### Step by step

1. Name the work situation and the expected result exactly. 2. Write down the mandatory limits (time frame, scope, style, prohibitions). 3. Request the first AI version in a specific format. 4. Run a 3-point quality gate, paying special attention to the risk of **mixing of points of view**. 5. Fix the output, then save it as a usable template.

### Do / Don't

**Do**
- Enter a short, specific task description so that the format of the comparative decision table is clear from the start.
- Before finalizing, check whether at least 4 common evaluation criterion measurement points have been met.


**Don't**
- Don't leave the topic of mixing aspects in the background, even if the answer seems good at first.
- Don't ask for a general text if you need material for a specific decision for the task of comparing 3 supplier offers.

### Common errors and their correction

- **Error:** an overly general request is made for the situation of comparing 3 supplier offers. **Correction:** name the person concerned, the deadline and the decision target.
- **Error:** there is no connection between the output and at least 4 common evaluation criteria measurements. **Fix:** record 1 binary pass/fail result at the end of each run.

### It's ready if

- [ ] You have completed and saved **comparative decision table** output.
